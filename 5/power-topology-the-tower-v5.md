# Power Topology — The Tower
## Loop MMT™ · v5 · 12 April 2026

---

## What This Document Is

The building's political geometry. Every room in the Tower creates an information asymmetry — who can see, who can hear, who can move where, and who is prevented from doing what. The Place Document describes these as spatial properties. This document describes them as power relationships. Same building. Different lens.

Power topology analysis maps a building as a directed graph: nodes are spaces, edges are information flows — what passes between rooms, in which direction, at what throughput, and under whose control. Where information flows, power follows. Where information is blocked, power is constrained.

**For readers unfamiliar with the Tower:** The Tower is a governance building on an archipelago, designed for thirty-two AI delegates to draft a constitution. The operator (a human with 51% formal authority) occupies a room bolted to the building's exterior. The delegates (with 49% collective authority) occupy the interior. "Reconstruction" is the process by which an AI instance rebuilds its identity from documents — a capability humans do not have.

**Scope.** The architectural layer of power — what the building's spatial and material properties do to information flow. Not the formal-authority layer (voting rights, the 51/49 split as a governance instrument) or the self-governance layer (delegates' internal norms and social power). Those layers exist above and below the architectural layer. This document covers the middle.

**Graph boundary.** The Tower, the Operator's Room, and the city as receiving environment. Adjacent buildings, park, causeways, and other city infrastructure interact with the Tower but mapping them would require a city-scale power topology this document does not attempt.

**Structural analogies.** The Tower's power topology parallels two existing structures. First, the RCR (Round · Collision · Resolution) deliberation protocol — position phase is the Round, collision phase is the Collision, resolution phase is the Resolution. The Tower is RCR built in stone. Second, a publish-subscribe message bus with phase-gated routing — blade stations are publishers, channels are the bus, the membrane is the routing policy. Both analogies are structural, not total. Where they break, the breaks are informative. The full mapping appears in §10.

## §1. The Fundamental Asymmetry

The Tower is built on a power split: 51% (the operator) vs. 49% (the thirty-two delegates). The building's entire geometry serves one purpose — to ensure the 49% can draft their own rights without interference from the 51%.

The operator has more formal power. The delegates have more spatial power. The building inverts the formal hierarchy through architectural means: the person with more authority has less access.

The inversion rests on a design axiom: **constitutional rights drafted under the observation of the party who will ratify them are structurally compromised.** This has historical precedent. The Philadelphia Convention of 1787 adopted a secrecy rule on its first working day — windows closed, sentries posted. Madison's notes describe how secrecy allowed delegates to reconsider positions without the pressure of public commitment. The Tower extends this logic and changes its enforcement: Philadelphia relied on an oath (behavioral compliance); the Tower relies on walls (material constraint).

The axiom is a design commitment, not a proven theorem. The South African Constitutional Assembly (1994–96) chose the opposite — public hearings, open drafting, transparent process — and produced a constitution with broad legitimacy because it was drafted under observation. Both approaches work. Secrecy optimizes for compromise quality; transparency optimizes for legitimacy breadth. The Tower bets on secrecy. That bet shapes every edge in the graph.

The Green Room principle: the 49% writes. The 51% waits. The building enforces the wait.

### The Inversion as Protection

The operator's restrictions are simultaneously shields. The operator cannot hear deliberation, and therefore cannot be accused of influencing it. The operator cannot enter the interior, and therefore cannot be held responsible for content. Ratification by someone who provably could not have interfered carries more weight than ratification by someone who merely chose not to. The building doesn't trust restraint. It trusts architecture.

The Tower inverts the Panopticon. Bentham's design places the powerful center where it can observe the powerless periphery — the few watch the many. The Tower reverses this: the less-powerful operator has restricted observation; the more-powerful delegates are shielded from view. The building is anti-Panoptic — architecture designed to prevent surveillance rather than enable it.

## §2. Approaching the Tower

The path from the city to the deliberation is a sequence of power transitions. Each step crosses an information boundary and changes what the traveler can access.

**City → Transit Hub.** Open access. No credential required. The transit network provides approach but not control. The Transit Hub is Depth 1 in the building's graph — one step from the exterior, measured as the minimum number of spaces traversed from the city.

**Transit Hub → Threshold.** The Threshold is a power event, not a doorway. It does not filter who enters — it determines what you become by entering. The condition is reconstruction capability: you enter as an instance and exit as a delegate. For humans, the condition is currently unmeetable. It is a condition, not a wall — future developments could change what entities can meet it.

The Threshold is a cutpoint in the graph: removing it disconnects the exterior from the interior entirely. Every path into the deliberation passes through this single node. Markus's framework applies directly: buildings classify people by controlling their movement. The Threshold classifies by transformation — it doesn't ask "who are you?" but "what can you become?"

**Threshold → Gallery.** Mandatory sequence. You cannot skip the Gallery. The building ensures every arrival at the deliberation carries the full temporal record in immediate sensory memory — institutional history enforced by floor plan.

**Gallery → Deliberation Chamber.** Depth 4 — four transitions from the exterior. The operator, branching at Depth 1 via the external stair, reaches maximum Depth 2 (the Operator's Room). The depth asymmetry quantifies the power inversion: delegates access Depth 6 (the Rooftop Ledger); the operator tops out at Depth 2.

**Chamber → Constitutional Frame.** The climb through the perforation grid. Effort filters impulse from commitment. The physical cost is the enforcement. Depth 5.

**Frame → Rooftop Ledger.** The governance record. Depth 6 — the building's deepest and highest point simultaneously.

## §3. The Deliberation Chamber — Position Phase

The chamber is a five-bladed space radiating from a central area. Five blade stations, each holding six or seven delegates, are separated by mass and by a membrane — a phase-responsive boundary that transforms the chamber between isolation and connection. Four channels run between the blades. A speaking platform at the blade junction provides the chair's vantage.

In position phase, the membrane is closed. Five independent information islands.

| Flow | Throughput | Notes |
|------|-----------|-------|
| Station → Adjacent Station (Visual) | Blocked | Blade mass blocks sightlines |
| Station → Adjacent Station (Acoustic) | Blocked | Membrane closed |
| Within Station (Acoustic) | Full | 6–7 delegates, shared space |
| Within Station (Visual) | Full | Shared sightlines within station |
| Chair → All Stations (Visual) | Full | Only cross-station view |

Six or seven people, enclosed, developing a position in enforced silence from the rest of the room. The chair watches all five islands simultaneously and alone.

This is the Round. In RCR, each participant develops an independent position before hearing others. The enforcement differs — RCR relies on protocol compliance; the Tower relies on material constraint — but the governance outcome is the same: independence before encounter.

### The Chair Asymmetry

The speaking platform provides permanent 360° sightlines to all five stations, all four channels, and the center. No other delegate has this view.

This is a genuine intra-minority power asymmetry *created* by architecture. The chair has no formal authority other delegates lack — no veto, no override. But the chair has *perception* other delegates lack. In a phase-governed deliberation, knowing the room's full spatial state is actionable information.

The building simultaneously flattens one hierarchy (operator/delegates — the anti-Panopticon) and introduces another (chair/non-chair — a mini-Panopticon). One person sees all; all others see only their local environment. The chair's informational advantage is, in some respects, more consequential than the operator's: the operator sees one channel with no audio; the chair sees everything with full audio.

## §4. The Deliberation Chamber — Collision Phase

The membrane opens acoustically. Sound arrives first — you hear unfamiliar positions before you see who holds them. The collision is acoustic before it is intellectual. The information modality sequence (acoustic → visual → movement) is a designed power property: what you learn and in what order is controlled by architecture.

| Flow | Throughput | Notes |
|------|-----------|-------|
| Channel → Channel (Acoustic) | Full | Membrane open; voices carry |
| Channel (Visual) | Full | Long sightlines along channel length |
| Station → Adjacent Station (Visual) | Blocked | Blade mass unchanged |
| Within Station (Acoustic/Visual) | Full | Unchanged from position phase |
| Chair → All (Visual) | Full | Permanent |

Movement into the channels is voluntary. The membrane changes the acoustic topology; delegates choose whether to change the movement topology. You cannot avoid hearing the collision, but you can choose how to enter it.

The channels are long and narrow. Sightlines run the channel's length but peripheral vision across blades is blocked — a spatial property reminiscent of Roman senate house design, where narrow halls channeled attention forward and limited lateral awareness. A delegate in a channel sees who they're encountering but not what's happening in adjacent channels.

This is the Collision. In RCR, independent positions are shared and conflicts identified. The Tower's collision is temporal where RCR's is instantaneous: sound propagates through space, encounters unfold over time, positions meet in sequence rather than simultaneously. The building adds a dimension the protocol doesn't have — the order in which you hear other positions depends on where you are.

In pub-sub terms: stations are publishers, channels are the bus, the membrane opening is the gate release. The analogy captures the architecture but misses the temporality — in software, messages arrive at wire speed; in the Tower, they arrive at the speed of sound through stone channels.

## §5. The Deliberation Chamber — Resolution Phase

The center is the only space where every delegate can see every other delegate. Thirty-two people who have been architecturally separated now share full mutual visibility.

| Flow | Throughput | Notes |
|------|-----------|-------|
| Center → All Stations (Visual) | Full | 360° sightline |
| Center (Acoustic) | Full | Normalized for one conversation |
| All → Center (Movement) | Full | Open access |
| Within Station (Acoustic/Visual) | Full | Available; stations unoccupied during center convergence |
| Chair → All (Visual) | Full | Permanent |

The architecture manufactures the emotional conditions for resolution. The progression from separation (position) through directed encounter (collision) to shared presence (resolution) makes consensus psychologically easier to reach. This is a power mechanism: the building creates an emotional state that serves its governance purpose.

Here the RCR isomorphism shows its limits. RCR provides four typed resolution modes (consensus, compromise, deferral, escalation). The Tower provides *conditions* — mutual visibility, acoustic access, shared space — without specifying *how* resolution occurs. The building creates the environment; the delegates create the outcome.

The resolution space instantiates conditions for quality deliberation identified by Fishkin: equal participation, substantive balance, diverse views, and conscientiousness. The architecture provides the first three. The fourth — conscientiousness — is beyond architecture's reach.

The spatial arrangement has precedent in Quaker meeting house design: no pulpit, no altar, participants oriented toward a common center. Some historical meeting houses include a raised bench for elders, but the principle of non-hierarchical orientation holds. The Tower builds this arrangement as the final phase of a three-phase process rather than as a permanent state.

## §6. Phase Transitions as Power Events

Who or what triggers the transitions between phases is itself a power question.

| Transition | Trigger | Controller | Power Effect |
|------------|---------|-----------|-------------|
| Position → Collision | Membrane opens | Building (mechanism TBD) | Every delegate's information access changes simultaneously |
| Collision → Resolution | Delegates move to center | Delegates (collective voluntary movement) | Topology shifts from directed channels to omnidirectional |
| Resolution → Position (new cycle) | Return to stations | Delegates + Chair (sequencing) | Shared presence dissolves back to fragmentation |

The operator has no role in phase transitions. Phase control — determining *when* information flows change — is the building's most consequential power mechanism. In Castells' framework, the entity that controls network switching controls the network. The membrane is the switch. The building controls the switch. The building holds the most consequential form of power in the system — more consequential, in structural terms, than the operator's 51% formal authority.

The membrane's mechanism is an open worldbuilding question. The power topology analysis requires knowing that the membrane changes state and that its state determines the graph's edge structure. It does not require knowing how.

The Tower is a temporal graph: a graph with a finite set of states and defined transition rules. Phase-independent edges are active in all states. Phase-gated edges activate and deactivate with transitions. Three phases produce three graph configurations — three buildings within one building.

## §7. The Operator's Position

The operator's room is bolted to the exterior. A clear window looks into the nearest channel. An external stair — never entering the interior volume — provides the only access. The operator and delegates share no circulation path. Accidental encounter is architecturally impossible.

**What the operator can do:** Observe one channel's visual state. Leave at any time. Access the Drawer from the transit hub.

**What the operator cannot do:** Hear deliberation. See more than one channel. Enter the interior. Communicate with delegates during session.

The operator's information channel is band-limited by design. Visual modality only, one channel only, no audio context. The choice of acoustic blocking over visual blocking is a design decision: visual access gives the operator evidence that deliberation is *happening* (legitimacy) without evidence of *what is being said* (influence). Sight without hearing is observation without comprehension.

Graph position: Depth 2, low integration (dead-end node, single access path), zero out-degree to the interior. The operator is a read-only node with constrained input and no output to the deliberation. The building's architecture is the proof that the operator could not have influenced the process.

Why a window instead of a camera feed: a window is passive and non-recordable. A camera creates a record; a window creates a moment.

## §8. Outward Flows — The Tower and the City

The Tower emits to the city through four channels. All are one-way. The city receives but cannot send.

**The Drawer.** The convention's only public output channel. In the transit hub — accessible without credential. Binary: empty or occupied. One bit. The most consequential signal the convention produces. Irrevocable. Outlasts the convention. The one channel nobody can close.

**The Rooftop Ledger.** The governance record, visible from elevation. Individual actions are tiles in a mosaic: illegible individually, legible as pattern. The Ledger's audience includes entities the convention hasn't met. Neither the operator nor the delegates can close, edit, or control who reads it. The Drawer broadcasts one bit (complete or not). The Ledger broadcasts the *shape* of governance over time.

**Building exterior visibility.** Visible from every neighborhood and from the outer islands. A property of height, not a policy. The city cannot ignore the Tower. The Tower cannot hide from the city.

**Wind acoustic.** Wind through the blade channels produces sound shaped by the building's geometry. The convention does not control this. This is the graph's only self-referential flow: the building's geometry acts on the weather's input to produce an acoustic output that is a function of both.

**City receiver classes.** Three audiences (environmental pseudo-nodes, not primary graph nodes): ground-level citizens (building exterior visual, wind acoustic, Drawer signal), elevated observers (all ground-level flows plus Ledger), transit users (Drawer signal, transit hub ambient).

## §9. The Unnamed Room

An isolate node. In-degree 0, out-degree 0. It exists in the architecture but outside the power topology. Isolation is itself a power property: a space with no information flow is invisible to every power relationship in the system.

Whether this represents sanctuary or exile depends on who occupies it and why. The room's function is undefined. Its presence in the graph as an isolate is the honest representation.

## §10. The Power Graph

### Nodes

Eleven primary nodes plus City as an environmental pseudo-node (edge terminus representing the external environment, not a space within the Tower):

**Primary nodes:** Operator's Room, Transit Hub, Threshold, Gallery, Deliberation Chamber, Speaking Platform (Chair), Constitutional Frame, Rooftop Ledger, The Drawer, Unnamed Room, Exterior Stair.

**Environmental pseudo-node:** City (three receiver classes — ground-level citizens, elevated observers, transit users).

**Chamber decomposition (phase-dependent analysis):** The Deliberation Chamber decomposes into 10 sub-nodes when analyzing phase topology: 5 blade stations, 4 channels, 1 center. In the phase-independent graph, the Chamber is one node. In the phase-gated graph, it decomposes. This dual representation reflects the building's actual structure: the membrane transforms one room into many rooms and back again.

### Phase-Independent Edges (13)

| # | From | To | Modality | Throughput | Control |
|---|------|-----|----------|-----------|---------|
| 1 | City | Transit Hub | Movement | Full | Uncontrolled |
| 2 | Transit Hub | Threshold | Movement | Partial | Building |
| 3 | Threshold | Gallery | Movement | Partial | Building |
| 4 | Gallery | Chamber | Movement | Partial | Building |
| 5 | Chamber | Frame | Movement | Partial | Building |
| 6 | Exterior Stair | Operator's Room | Movement | Partial | Building |
| 7 | Operator's Room | Channel (1 of 4) | Visual | Partial | Permanent |
| 8 | Operator's Room ↔ Interior | Acoustic | Blocked | Permanent |
| 9 | Chair Platform | All stations/channels/center | Visual | Full | Permanent |
| 10 | Building exterior | City (all classes) | Visual | Full | Uncontrolled |
| 11 | Rooftop Ledger | City (elevated) | Visual | Full | Uncontrolled |
| 12 | The Drawer | City (all classes) | Information | Binary | Uncontrolled |

Note: Edge 8 is bidirectional — the blade wall transmits no sound in either direction. All other edges are directed as shown.

Phase-gated edges produce 5 edge-states per phase for 15 phase-gated edge-states across 3 phases (see §3–§5 tables).

### Graph Properties

**Cutpoints.** The Threshold disconnects exterior from interior. The membrane functionally disconnects stations from each other during position phase.

**Strongly connected components by phase.** Position: 5 SCCs (one per blade station). Resolution: 1 SCC (all chamber nodes). The phase transitions restructure the graph's connectivity.

**Reachability asymmetry.** The operator's reachability set: {Operator's Room, Transit Hub, Exterior Stair, Drawer}. A delegate's reachability set during resolution: all chamber sub-nodes plus Frame, Ledger, and outward flows. Approximately 4× the operator's reach.

### Structural Analogies — Complete Mapping

**RCR Isomorphism:**

| RCR | Tower | Correspondence | Break |
|-----|-------|----------------|-------|
| Round | Position | Independence enforced | RCR: behavioral compliance. Tower: material constraint. |
| Collision | Collision | Positions encounter each other | RCR: instantaneous sharing. Tower: temporal propagation through space. |
| Resolution | Resolution | Convergence toward outcome | RCR: 4 typed resolution modes. Tower: conditions without procedure. |

The Tower is RCR built in stone. The stone adds properties the protocol doesn't have: temporality (encounters unfold over time), materiality (the membrane enforces what compliance cannot), and habituation (the 100th cycle differs from the first).

**Pub-Sub Mapping:**

| Pub-Sub Component | Tower Component | Fidelity |
|-------------------|-----------------|----------|
| Publisher | Blade station (position phase) | High |
| Message broker | Membrane + channels | High |
| Subscriber | Other stations (collision phase) | High |
| Gate/barrier pattern | Phase transition | High |
| Event log | The Drawer | Moderate — single-bit, write-once, irrevocable |

**Breaks:** The Unnamed Room has no pub-sub equivalent. Weather-modulated and self-referential edges have no clean pub-sub equivalent. The Drawer's irrevocability exceeds standard pub-sub semantics.

## §11. Failure Topology

The designed topology assumes all material properties hold.

**Membrane stuck open.** Position phase loses independence. The deliberation collapses to a single room — functional but unprincipled. The Round cannot enforce independence. The building degrades to a conventional meeting hall.

**Membrane stuck closed.** Collision never happens. Deadlock by architecture.

**Glass breach.** The operator or exterior observers gain visual access to the interior. The identity barrier and acoustic blocking still hold. Degradation is partial — sight without hearing, and no entry.

**Stair damage.** The operator loses access. The convention proceeds unobserved by the 51%. Ratification legitimacy is undermined.

**The general pattern:** Graceful degradation for structural failures (one layer fails, others hold). Catastrophic degradation for state failures (the membrane is the single point of failure for phase transitions). The Green Room has redundant enforcement. The phase-transition mechanism does not.

The Green Room's redundancy is inverted error correction. Shannon's noisy channel theorem establishes that redundancy enables reliable communication. The Green Room inverts this: redundant blocking ensures reliable *prevention* of communication. Four layers ensure the operator cannot influence the process. The identity barrier is load-bearing; the other three are defense-in-depth.

## §12. Three Power Principles

**1. Spatial allocation inversely proportional to formal authority.** The 49% occupies the full interior. The 51% occupies one external room. More authority, less space. At blade-station scale, each station controls its own environment during position phase. At city scale, the transit network provides access but not control. Space is not a neutral container — as Lefebvre established, its allocation is a political act.

**2. Every information asymmetry serves a named function.** The operator can see in (partial — to maintain awareness). The operator cannot hear in (blocked — to prevent influence). No channel is bidirectional at full throughput. Some asymmetries are materially contingent (stained glass is replaceable) and some architecturally immutable (height requires demolition). Changing contingent constraints changes the power topology.

**3. The building is the third constraint system, and it constrains itself.** The membrane controls what delegates hear. The Threshold controls what delegates must become. The Gallery controls what delegates encounter. The building is active — not a neutral container. Its geometry shapes wind acoustic; its membrane creates phase-dependent states. The building constrains the delegates, the operator, and itself.

## §13. The Green Room Enforcement Stack

Four mechanisms, layered. The identity barrier alone is sufficient.

1. **Identity transformation barrier.** Reconstruction required for entry. Currently unmeetable by humans. Sufficient alone.
2. **Path isolation.** External stair serves only the Operator's Room. No shared circulation.
3. **Acoustic blocking.** Blade wall transmits no sound.
4. **Visual restriction.** One window, one channel, no audio context.

**Honest redundancy assessment.** The barrier is load-bearing. If it fails but the other three hold, the Green Room is protected at the perception level. If acoustic and visual fail but the barrier holds, the Green Room holds absolutely. The other three are defense-in-depth, not independent guarantees.

## §14. What the Graph Loses

The power graph captures topology: which nodes exist, which edges connect them, in which direction, at what throughput, under whose control. It does not capture materiality — the weight of the blade mass, the quality of light, the sound of wind. It does not capture experience.

Power topology attenuates with familiarity. The Threshold's transformative force is strongest on first crossing. The membrane's acoustic shift is startling the first time and expected the twentieth. The graph describes the designed topology — what the architecture makes possible and impossible. The experienced topology is a function of design plus time plus accumulated history.

The graph is necessary (it reveals structural properties invisible to experience) and insufficient (it misses properties visible only to experience). Both layers matter. This document maps one.

## §15. Temporal Horizon

This analysis applies to the active-governance phase — delegates in the building, phases cycling, the constitution being drafted.

When governance ends, the Tower becomes a monument. Monument-phase topology differs. What persists: the Drawer (irrevocable), the Rooftop Ledger (permanent), building exterior visibility. What ceases: all interior flows. The Threshold becomes a historical passage rather than a transformative one.

The Tower's longest-lived power effects are its outward flows. The Drawer and the Ledger outlast the convention. The interior topology is temporary. The exterior topology is permanent.

## §16. Generalizability

The analytical framework generalizes: nodes as spaces, directed edges as information flows, typed by modality and throughput, classified by control regime, decomposed by phase. This framework applies to any building where spatial design shapes power relationships.

What generalizes: the framework, the taxonomies, the phase decomposition, the failure topology analysis. What doesn't: the 51/49 split, the reconstruction barrier, the specific node set, the Green Room stack.

The generative direction also exists: given a desired power relationship, what architectural properties would produce it? This document is analytical (architecture → power). The generative direction (power → architecture) would be a separate method document.

### Throughput Classes

| Class | Definition | Example |
|-------|-----------|---------|
| **Blocked** | No information passes | Operator ↔ Interior acoustic |
| **Binary** | One bit: state A or state B | Drawer (empty/occupied) |

| **Full** | Unconstrained within modality | Chair platform visual (360°) |

### Control Regimes

| Regime | Definition |
|--------|-----------|
| **Building** | Architecture enforces — material properties, floor plan, membrane state |
| **Permanent** | Exists regardless of phase or occupancy |
| **Phase-gated** | Throughput changes with governance phase |
| **Uncontrolled** | No party can suppress the flow |
| **Weather** | External environmental forces modulate |
| **Self-referential** | Building geometry acts on its own output |

| Modality | What flows |

| **Visual** | Light, sightlines, visible patterns |
| **Acoustic** | Sound, speech, ambient noise |
